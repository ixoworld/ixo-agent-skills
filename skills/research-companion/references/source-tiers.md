# Source Tiers - Extended Guide

Use to decide how much weight to give a source and how to label it in the brief.

## Tier 1 - Primary

The strongest evidence. The thing itself, not a summary of the thing.

| Field | Examples |
|---|---|
| Science / medicine | Peer-reviewed journal articles, preprints from established venues (arXiv, bioRxiv), cohort data, registered trials |
| Law / regulation | The statute or regulation itself, court decisions, regulatory guidance, official agency releases |
| Business / finance | 10-K, 10-Q, S-1 filings, earnings transcripts, central bank releases, official statistics offices |
| Tech / product | Official documentation, RFCs, source code, official changelogs and release notes |
| History / archive | Primary documents, period news articles, contemporaneous correspondence |

Use without hedging. Date-stamp.

## Tier 2 - Reputable secondary

Established outlets and analysts with editorial standards and a track record. Strong but not gospel.

| Field | Examples |
|---|---|
| General news | FT, Reuters, AP, BBC, NYT, WSJ, The Economist |
| Industry / trade | Established trade press (e.g., MIT Tech Review, Variety, Lancet news section) |
| Analysts / research firms | Gartner, Forrester, McKinsey reports, Pew Research |
| Encyclopedic | Britannica, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Cochrane reviews |

Cite without apology, but check the date and check whether the source is reporting a primary finding (good) or rehashing another secondary source (weaker).

## Tier 3 - Popular secondary

Useful for context and pointers to primary sources, but treat the substantive claim as needing further verification.

| Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| Major blogs | Stratechery, Marginal Revolution, individual columns on Substack with track record |
| Mainstream tech press | The Verge, TechCrunch, Ars Technica |
| Podcasts and interviews | Quote the speaker, name the show and date |
| Wikipedia | Useful for orientation; cite its sources rather than Wikipedia itself |

Cite, but pair with a stronger source if the claim is load-bearing.

## Tier 4 - Weak

Use only when nothing better exists. Always label.

- Reddit threads, Hacker News comments, Twitter/X posts (unless the poster is the primary actor)
- Anonymous blog posts
- AI-generated summaries
- Marketing pages and vendor white papers (for claims about the vendor's own product, treat as primary but biased)
- Forum posts with no author identification

When using weak sources, write: "One forum thread reports X [source]. This is not corroborated elsewhere I could find."

## Special cases

- **Vendor's own claims about their own product**: primary (they wrote it) but biased (they're selling it). Cite as "[Vendor] claims..." not "It is the case that...".
- **Wikipedia**: scan it for orientation and follow its citations to the actual source. Cite the underlying source.
- **News aggregators / SEO content farms**: do not cite. Find the underlying source they cribbed from.
- **AI-generated content**: do not cite. Treat as unverified.

## Rule of thumb

A briefing that rests entirely on Tier 3-4 sources is not a briefing - it's a guess in a suit. Either find Tier 1-2 sources or say "I could not find strong sources for this."
